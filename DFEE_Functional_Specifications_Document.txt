Deterministic Film Emulation EngineDetailed Functional Specifications Document
Version 1.0 | Value-Based Parametric Film Response Architecture

Prepared as a functional specification for a deterministic RAW image processing system that emulates analog film behavior using measurable image-state parameters: exposure, luminance, hue, saturation, channel balance, density, contrast, spatial frequency, grain receptivity, and optical highlight behavior.
1. Executive Summary
The Deterministic Film Emulation Engine (DFEE) is a RAW-first image rendering system designed to transform digital camera images into film-like photographs through a deterministic, scene-adaptive, value-based processing pipeline. The system does not depend on semantic object recognition as its primary logic. It instead emulates how film reacts to light, density, hue, saturation, contrast, color-channel exposure, highlight stress, shadow depth, grain visibility, and optical scattering.
The core product problem is that Lightroom presets and conventional LUTs assume the input image is within a narrow working range. When the RAW file has unusual exposure, camera-specific color bias, harsh highlights, deep shadows, or oversaturated digital colors, a fixed preset may overcorrect, clip, shift, or flatten the image. DFEE addresses this by first extracting a detailed image-state profile and then computing a render plan that applies a selected film stock profile proportionally and safely.
1.1 Core Principle
Film does not know objects. It responds to incoming light. Therefore, DFEE shall emulate film through luminance, chroma, hue, density, channel response, contrast, and spatial-frequency behavior rather than through object-specific edits.
1.2 Source Basis and Extension
The initial reference architecture proposed RAW scene-linear ingestion, continuous luminance-zone masks, dynamic range assessment, grain receptivity maps, specular halation triggers, and edge-gradient maps. This specification preserves those valid principles while expanding them into a complete feature extraction, film-response, parameter-solver, and rendering pipeline.

2. Product Goals and Non-Goals
2.1 Product Goals
Produce film-like outputs from RAW digital images without relying on fixed presets or fixed LUTs as the primary rendering mechanism.
Analyze each image before transformation to understand exposure, tonal distribution, color cast, hue distribution, saturation pressure, channel clipping, shadow density, highlight stress, texture, and grain receptivity.
Apply film stock behavior as a controlled response system across luminance zones, hue bands, saturation ranges, and spatial-frequency regions.
Maintain deterministic reproducibility: the same input RAW, selected profile, version, and user controls shall produce the same output.
Allow camera-specific biases, such as cool/blue shadow casts or green-magenta imbalance, to be normalized before or during film rendering.
Produce an explainable render report showing input diagnosis and applied rendering decisions.
Support future extension to multiple film stock styles, scanner styles, and print styles without rewriting the core engine.
2.2 Product Non-Goals
DFEE shall not primarily rely on AI object detection, segmentation, or generative image manipulation to create the film look.
DFEE shall not hallucinate new image content, replace regions, or create semantic edits such as changing sky, skin, face, clothes, or background independently as a core operation.
DFEE shall not treat film emulation as a single global HSL adjustment, global LUT, or fixed preset stack.
DFEE shall not attempt exact legal or brand-claimed replication of commercial film stocks unless supported by properly licensed calibration data and branding permissions.
DFEE shall not assume that all photos should be corrected to a neutral, technically perfect baseline before rendering; film aesthetics may intentionally preserve warmth, darkness, or imperfect exposure.
3. System Overview
3.1 End-to-End Functional Flow
1. Ingest RAW image and metadata.
2. Convert to scene-linear working representation.
3. Compute luminance, chroma, hue, saturation, channel, and spatial-frequency diagnostics.
4. Estimate camera/input color bias and dynamic range state.
5. Generate continuous luminance-zone masks and value-based control maps.
6. Combine image-state features with selected film stock and scan/print profile.
7. Generate deterministic adaptive render plan.
8. Apply exposure placement, film response curves, channel-density behavior, hue/saturation compression, local contrast shaping, grain synthesis, halation/bloom, and scanner/print finishing.
9. Export rendered image and sidecar render report.

6. Film Stock Profile Requirements
6.1 Film Stock as Behavior
A film profile shall define behavior, not fixed slider values. The selected stock determines how tonal zones, hue bands, saturation ranges, channel relationships, grain, halation, and scanner/print finishing behave. The image-state analysis determines how strongly those behaviors should be applied.
Profile Area
Required Parameters
Tone response
toe strength, toe length, midtone slope, shoulder strength, highlight rolloff, black density floor, white density ceiling.
Channel response
red, green, and blue curve offsets; channel-specific shoulder; shadow and highlight channel bias.
Hue behavior
hue shifts and compression rules for red/orange, yellow, green, cyan, blue, magenta, and neutral ranges.
Saturation behavior
low-saturation lift, high-saturation compression, highlight desaturation, shadow chroma reduction, neon compression.
Density behavior
density build-up by zone, density-to-chroma interaction, midtone weight, shadow density handling.
Grain behavior
grain size, strength, chroma grain, luminance peak zone, shadow/highlight grain limits, texture masking.
Halation and bloom
specular threshold, radius, falloff, red/orange bias, receiver mask logic, bloom strength.
Scanner/print finish
contrast, warmth, black point, white point, color separation, sharpening/softening, scan style.

7. Parameter Solver Requirements
7.1 Solver Objective
The parameter solver shall combine the input image-state feature dictionary with the selected film stock profile and scan/print profile to generate a deterministic render plan. The solver is responsible for deciding how strongly to apply each stock behavior based on the actual starting condition of the image.
7.2 Solver Logic
Input Condition
Solver Response Example
Image already has cool blue shadows.
Reduce accidental blue/cyan bias before applying stock shadow color.
Image has limited highlight headroom.
Increase shoulder compression and highlight desaturation.
Image is flat and overcast.
Increase midtone density and local contrast while protecting highlight softness.
Image has high neon saturation.
Increase hue-specific saturation compression and reduce out-of-gamut risk.
Image has point light sources.
Increase halation only if local contrast and receiver-density masks support it.
Image has large diffuse highlights.
Use shoulder and bloom; suppress halation.
Image has underexposed noisy shadows.

8. Rendering Engine Functional Modules
8.1 Module RE-01: Pre-Film Normalization
This module prepares the image for film rendering without making it sterile or over-corrected. It shall handle exposure placement, camera cast normalization, channel clipping repair where possible, and noise-risk protection.
Function
Description
Controls
Exposure placement
Reposition midtones using percentile anchors and stock target density.
exposure_compensation_stops, midtone_anchor_target.
Camera cast normalization
Reduce accidental camera or white-balance bias before stock color behavior.
neutral_confidence, shadow_cast_correction, blue_excess_control.
Highlight repair
Recover or soften clipped channel relationships where possible.
channel_recovery_strength, shoulder_preconditioning.
Noise-risk protection
Prevent shadow lifting from revealing digital noise.
shadow_noise_limit, pre_grain_denoise_strength.

8.2 Module RE-02: Film Tone Response
This module applies the selected stock’s toe, midtone slope, and shoulder behavior in a log-exposure or density-like domain. The response may be channel-specific, but hue integrity must be protected from unstable shifts.
Apply toe compression to deep shadows and shadows.
Apply midtone density and contrast according to selected stock.
Apply highlight shoulder and white-density compression based on highlight headroom.
Support separate red, green, and blue channel response curves for film-like color behavior.
Preserve proportional chromaticity during pure luminance remapping unless stock profile explicitly modifies color response.
8.3 Module RE-03: Zone × Hue × Saturation Color Response
This is the core color module. The system shall apply stock-specific behavior based on the intersection of luminance zone, hue band, and saturation/chroma level.
Dimension
Examples of Behavior
Luminance zone
Deep shadows lose chroma; upper midtones soften; highlights desaturate and roll off.
Hue band
Cyan/blue may be softened; greens may shift olive or fresh; red/orange may be compressed to avoid digital skin/warm-light clipping.
Saturation range
Low saturation may receive gentle color separation; high saturation is compressed; neon colors are strongly controlled.
Channel state
Existing channel imbalance may reduce or increase the strength of stock-specific shifts.

8.4 Module RE-04: Density and Color Volume Compression
This module prevents digitally harsh color behavior after tone and color response. It shall compress extreme chroma according to hue, luminance, and film stock density behavior, rather than applying a global saturation slider.
Compress high-chroma blues/cyans in highlights and upper midtones when required.
Compress red/orange in midtones if the source is already warm or channel-stressed.
Mute or reshape green/yellow ranges according to stock behavior.
Apply highlight desaturation proportional to shoulder compression.
Prevent output gamut clipping by using perceptual color-volume compression.
8.5 Module RE-05: Local Contrast and Acutance Shaping
Film character includes a different relationship between global contrast, local contrast, perceived sharpness, grain, and edge softness. This module shall use low-, mid-, and high-frequency decomposition to shape contrast without creating digital halos.
Frequency Band
Treatment
Low frequency
Controls global density and large-area contrast.
Mid frequency
Controls object separation and film-like presence.
High frequency
Controls brittle digital sharpness, texture, and grain interaction.
Edges
Supports adjacency effects, soft bloom boundaries, and acutance moderation.

8.6 Module RE-06: Procedural Film Grain
Grain shall be generated procedurally and modulated by image density, spatial frequency, output resolution, and stock profile. It shall not be applied as a simple static overlay.
Generate grain in a density-like or perceptually stable domain.
Use separate luminance and subtle chroma grain components.
Modulate grain by luminance zone; typically strongest in lower midtones/midtones depending on stock.
Mask grain in high-texture regions and reveal it in smooth gradients.
Scale grain size and frequency to output resolution.
Use deterministic random seeds derived from image hash, profile ID, and engine version unless user selects a custom seed.
8.7 Module RE-07: Halation, Bloom, and Optical Scatter
Halation shall be driven by value-based optical conditions rather than global highlight brightness. It should appear around localized, high-intensity, high-contrast highlights and bleed into adjacent lower-density areas.
Effect
Trigger
Output Behavior
Halation
Specular source × local contrast × receiver darkness.
Warm/red-orange bleed with falloff and stock-specific radius.
Bloom
Bright area × lens/scan softness × highlight stress.
Soft glow without necessarily red fringe.
Veiling glare
Large bright region or strong light source.
Low-frequency contrast reduction near strong light.
Adjacency softness
Bright-dark boundary with high local contrast.
Subtle edge softening and density interaction.

8.8 Module RE-08: Scanner / Print Finish
The final film appearance depends on scan or print interpretation. The renderer shall allow stock and finishing profile to be selected independently.
Finish Profile
Characteristics
Clean lab scan
Neutral, controlled, low artifact, technically balanced.
Frontier-like soft scan
Warm, pleasing contrast, rounded highlights, accessible color.
Noritsu-like smooth scan
Smoother color separation, softer tonal transitions.
Darkroom print
Richer blacks, print density, slightly more organic contrast.
Editorial scan
Controlled color, restrained saturation, high-end publication feel.
Consumer nostalgic scan
Warmer, less technically perfect, stronger mood and character.

10. Processing Order and Data Flow
The order of operations matters. A poor order will produce fake color, clipped highlights, overlay-like grain, or artificial halation.
1. RAW decode and normalization.
2. Linear luminance, color, and channel diagnostics.
3. Camera/input bias estimation.
4. Exposure-zone and spatial mask generation.
5. Parameter solver and render plan generation.
6. Pre-film normalization: exposure, channel repair, and cast correction.
7. Film tone response in log-exposure/density-like domain.
8. Zone × hue × saturation color transform.
9. Density and color-volume compression.
10. Local contrast/acutance shaping.
11. Grain synthesis and integration.
12. Halation, bloom, glare, and adjacency effects.
13. Scanner/print finishing.
14. Output conversion and export.

17.2 Acceptance Criteria
Output should not show hard clipping introduced by the film transform unless the selected profile intentionally supports severe contrast.
Camera color cast correction should not over-neutralize scenes with low neutral-confidence scores.
Film stock identity should remain recognizable across different images while adaptation prevents destructive over-application.
Grain should appear integrated into image density, not as an overlay floating above the image.
Halation should be absent or minimal on large diffuse highlights and more visible near localized high-intensity sources.
Highly saturated colors should be compressed gracefully rather than clipped or globally desaturated.
The render report should explain the major decisions made by the solver.
Batch processing should produce consistent stock identity while allowing per-image tonal adaptation.

19. Key Design Decisions
Decision
Rationale
Use value-based analysis, not object-aware editing.
This better matches how film actually behaves and keeps the system deterministic and explainable.
Use RAW scene-linear data as the foundation.
Film response is exposure-driven; baked gamma images lose essential information.
Use continuous masks rather than binary selections.
Avoids posterization, hard transitions, and fake local edits.
Use stock behavior profiles rather than slider presets.
Allows the same film identity to adapt safely across different inputs.
Separate camera/input normalization from film response.
Prevents camera-specific bias from being amplified by film color transforms.
Separate film stock from scanner/print finish.
The perceived film look is film plus interpretation; this gives flexible creative control.
Generate render reports.
Improves debugging, calibration, trust, and repeatability.

21. Conclusion
DFEE should be built as a deterministic, adaptive film-response engine rather than a preset system. The core of the system is not semantic understanding of objects, but precise measurement of image state: exposure, luminance zones, hue distribution, saturation pressure, camera color bias, channel clipping, shadow depth, highlight stress, spatial frequency, texture, and optical-source structure. Film stock profiles then act as behavioral response models applied through a parameter solver.
This architecture allows each RAW image to receive an individualized film rendering plan while preserving repeatability, explainability, and consistent stock identity. It directly addresses the fundamental weakness of presets: fixed transformations applied to unpredictable inputs.
